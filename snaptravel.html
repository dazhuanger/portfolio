SnapTravel

SnapTravel is an online travel agency based in Toronto. I was a UX research and design intern in the product design team. I helped SnapTravel revamp its current checkout flow.

My roles:

User Flow Audit
Research
Data Analysis
Ideation

Problem

I conducted a flow audit on the current website of SnapTravel. The goal was to identify issues that users may encounter while using the product. In particular, as a first-time user of SnapTravel myself, I had some different insights from users who are already familiar with the website. The problem of backtracking was one of them.

Collapsible Cards

The current checkout flow has three steps: hotel details, guest details and payment information, organized by three collapsible cards. This works well on desktops but creates problems on mobile devices.

Backtracking

Before tapping on the final confirmation button, if users want to double-check if the information they entered was correct, they have to expand the hotel details card again. However, once the card is expanded, it can only be collapsed by clicking the main button because the triangle button on the top-right corner is not a toggle switch. This means that users have to go through the entire process again.

Analysis

Data Analysis

Having identified these possible pain points, I dived into the database to find evidence to support my assumption. The event tracker on Amplitude showed:

50% of users go from step 2 to step 1.

35% of users go from step 3 to step 2.

21% of users go from step 3 to step 1.

11% of users who opened the booking form didn't finish the booking and let the rate expired.

The result indicates that the backtracking problem does exist and it is hurting the conversion rate since at least 11% of users didn't finish the booking. So how might we solve this problem and increase the conversion rate?

Competitive Analysis

I conducted research on the structure of booking forms on SnapTravel's major competitors. There are mainly two types of organization schemes.

Multiple pages: Booking.com

The checkout flow is separated in different pages. A timeline is on top to show the steps. Price is shown consistently at the bottom of each page with different CTA on the button.

Single page with scrolling: Priceline.com

Everything is on the same page with empty forms to be filled. Forms change colour after completion. There is only one button for the final confirmation.

Both organization schemes keep key information such as price and date within user's viewpoint consistently. This kind of design reduces user's cognitive burden and avoids the backtracking problem, which interrupts the entire checkout flow.

Solutions

Recommendations

Based on the research findings, I made some design recommendations to solve this problem.

  • Fixing the malfunctioning triangle button

    • This quick remedy requires the least effort. Turning the button into a toggle switch will allow the user to have a quick sneak peek of the forms they filled. However, the backtracking problem would still exist.

  • Sticky summary cards

    • This option also keeps the current mechanism. Making the collapsed card sticky to persist key details would solve the backtracking problem.

Next Steps

Following up on the design recommendations, the product design team will collaborate with product managers to build prototypes and run AB testings to see if the modified design would increase the conversion rate.

Key Takeaways

If it's not broken, why fix it?

Every design decision needs to be supported by the research data. If there is no evidence shows the problem exists, don't waste time fixing it. Likewise, if you are not 100% sure that the modification is going to increase the conversion rate, don't do it either. Avoid adding features unless they are essential to the application. Always keep the business and user goals at the forefront of the design process.

Dig deeper!

Rigorous data-oriented research is critical. It sets the right direction for your design and makes it more convincing to other stakeholders.
