AR Navigation

This is a project in response to a design prompt of a UX research and design course for augmented reality. It focused on the design and evaluation of an AR navigation app for a food night market.

My roles:

Ideation
Prototyping
Data Analysis

Project Description

Our group designed two new interaction techniques and compared them with the existing technique to determine which one will be the best at getting the user from spot A to B. We also aimed to discover some usability problems of the AR navigation app through the usability test.

AR Prototypes

Long Line

The device is held by a user. The navigation line is drawn from the user’s current position to the specified destination and is placed approximately at waist-level.

Signpost

The device is held by the user. Signs are positioned at critical junctures along the user’s route and additionally includes start and finish.

Design Process

Current Technique

  • Local cues

    • The existing technique features local cues which focus primarily on user's next step. The arrows appear sequentially as the user moves around.

  • Positioning

    • Arrows are placed at floor-level. Users need to put the hand-held device at a lower position to trigger the visual cue.

Ideation & Modifications

How might we make the interaction effortless?

  • Static global cues

    • Position of the destination is always available. It provides continuous, trigger-free guidance and is not time-sensitive.

How might we add more discoverability to the app?

  • Waist / eye level

    • Visual cues are moved up to eye level to keep the user's head up, which allows the user to pay more attention to surroundings.

Evaluation

Experiment

  • Procedure

    • Between-subjects experiment

    Three participants

    Each perform all three interactions techniques in a different order

  • Success Criteria

    • Effectiveness: ability to complete the task

    Efficiency: speed at which the user completes the task

    Satisfaction: user’s experience throughout the duration of the task

    Learnability: time and effort required by a user to achieve competency

Results

  • Technique 1. Arrows

    • Difficulty noticing the navigational cues

    Overwhelmed cognitive capacity

    Limited global knowledge of the environment

  • Technique 2. Long Line

    • Lessened cognitive load

    Instant, concise understanding of environment

    Direct, unambiguous and effective

  • Technique 3. Signpost

    • Blocked field of vision

    Insufficient amount of information

    Lack of progress tracking

Reflection

This experiment would most ideally be conducted as a between-subjects experiment to avoid learning effects. But due to the outbreak of COVID-19, only one participant was recruited. The user spent significantly less time completing the last task than the first. It cannot be known with any certainty if this is due to the user’s familiarity with the AR environment (learning effects) or the efficacy of the navigation technique.

Another shortcoming of this experiment is its low external validity —though a night market was simulated in the AR app, it ultimately bears little resemblance to an actual night market. Stimulus like noises, lighting, various physical obstacles, and human interactions were not articulated in this simulation.

The last shortcoming is the limitation of the prototyping tool, Wiarframe. Same as all the other AR prototyping tools for designers, Wiarframe is still in its early stage, which greatly constrained the complexity of our design.
